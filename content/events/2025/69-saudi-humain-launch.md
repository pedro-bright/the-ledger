## Summary

Saudi Arabia's Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman launched HUMAIN, a wholly PIF-owned AI company, with $23 billion in announced technology partnerships, a $10 billion venture fund, and a target of 1.9 gigawatts of data center capacity by 2030.

## What Happened

On May 12, 2025, the Saudi Public Investment Fund announced the formation of HUMAIN — positioned as the Kingdom's flagship AI development vehicle. The company is wholly owned by PIF and chaired by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, giving it direct sovereign backing at the highest level of Saudi leadership. At launch, HUMAIN announced $23 billion in technology partnerships spanning NVIDIA, AMD, and Qualcomm for chip supply and infrastructure, along with agreements covering model development and cloud services.

HUMAIN also established a $10 billion venture fund to invest in AI companies globally, with a focus on startups willing to establish or expand Gulf operations. The company's infrastructure roadmap called for 1.9 gigawatts of data center capacity in Saudi Arabia by 2030 — a figure that would place it among the largest AI compute operators in the world outside the US hyperscalers. Saudi Aramco took a minority stake in the venture, signaling the integration of HUMAIN into the broader Vision 2030 economic diversification strategy. The timing was notably simultaneous with President Trump's Middle East tour, framing HUMAIN as part of a broader US-Saudi AI alignment.

## Why It Matters

HUMAIN represented the Gulf's most ambitious sovereign AI bet to date. The combination of sovereign wealth fund backing, direct royal oversight, hyperscale infrastructure commitments, and major US chip partnerships positioned Saudi Arabia not merely as a customer of AI but as a potential developer and exporter of AI capabilities. The venture also raised questions about the relationship between sovereign AI investment and US export control policy: the large Nvidia and AMD chip purchases implicated by HUMAIN's infrastructure plans required US government approval under existing export regulations, creating a diplomatic and commercial tension that would play out through 2025 and 2026.
