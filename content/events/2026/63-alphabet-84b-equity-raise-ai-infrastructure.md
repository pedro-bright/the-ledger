## Summary

On June 1, 2026, Alphabet Inc. announced a proposed equity offering package totaling $80 billion, citing "unprecedented customer demand" for its AI compute services that was exceeding available supply. The company upsized the raise to $84.75 billion at pricing on June 2. The package combined $30 billion in underwritten public offerings, a $40 billion at-the-market program commencing in the third quarter, and a $10 billion private placement with Berkshire Hathaway. Alphabet guided full-year 2026 capital expenditures to $180 to $190 billion, with further increases projected for 2027. The raise was among the largest single equity capital mobilizations for AI infrastructure by a public company to that date.

## What Happened

Alphabet's June 1 announcement described the raise as driven by "strong demand for its AI solutions and services from enterprises and consumers, at levels that are exceeding the company's available supply." The company's investor relations release listed four concurrent transactions: $15 billion in depositary shares representing newly issued mandatory convertible preferred stock (Series A and Series B, each at $50 per share); $15 billion in Class A Common Stock and Class C Capital Stock; a $40 billion at-the-market offering program to begin in the third quarter of 2026; and a $10 billion private placement with Berkshire Hathaway.

The Berkshire placement was structured as two equal tranches of $5 billion each: Class A shares at $351.81 per share and Class C shares at $348.20 per share, both below the prevailing market price on the announcement date. Berkshire Hathaway's participation — the conglomerate had historically been skeptical of technology capital spending — was framed in coverage as a signal that mainstream institutional capital was endorsing AI infrastructure as a durable investment thesis.

On June 2, Alphabet announced pricing and upsized the total equity capital raise from $80 billion to $84.75 billion, reflecting strong institutional demand for the underwritten portions. The offering of common stock and capital stock was expected to close June 4; the preferred stock tranches were expected to close June 5. The $40 billion ATM program would add to the total through open-market sales beginning in the third quarter.

Alphabet's 2026 capital expenditure guidance, issued alongside the raise, was $180 to $190 billion — a figure that would represent approximately double the company's 2024 capital expenditure run rate. The company said it expected 2027 capital expenditures to "significantly increase" further. Bloomberg noted the raise followed "unprecedented customer demand" for Google Cloud compute and that capacity constraints were the immediate constraint on Alphabet's AI revenue growth.

The SEC filings accompanying the raise — Form FWP and Form 8-K filings posted to EDGAR — confirmed the pricing, share counts, and closing schedule. Alphabet's press release identified the use of proceeds as general corporate purposes including "capital expenditures to scale AI infrastructure and global compute," with no specific project or geography disclosed.

## Why It Matters

The $84.75 billion raise documents the scale at which a major technology company was willing to dilute existing shareholders in order to fund AI compute buildout — an unusual choice for a profitable public company with substantial free cash flow. Alphabet generated approximately $87 billion in operating cash flow in 2025; the equity raise represented roughly equivalent magnitude, indicating the company believed its AI infrastructure investment requirements exceeded what organic cash generation could fund without sacrificing pace.

The raise sits within a documented pattern from the first half of 2026: OpenAI's $122 billion private round, Anthropic's $65 billion Series H, the Apollo-Blackstone $35 billion debt package for Anthropic chip leasing, and now Alphabet's $84.75 billion public equity offering. Collectively, these transactions — each justified on the basis of AI compute demand — constitute a distinctive episode in capital markets history in which the premise of continued AI capability scaling attracted institutional capital at historically unusual scale and velocity.

The Berkshire Hathaway anchor investment merits specific note. Berkshire's prior technology investments had been concentrated in Apple and a small number of financial technology positions; Warren Buffett had publicly expressed skepticism about AI investment cycles at earlier points. A $10 billion direct placement in Alphabet's AI infrastructure raise, at below-market prices, represented a departure from that stated caution and was widely read in coverage as a de facto endorsement of the AI infrastructure thesis by the most prominent value investor in the United States. Whether the endorsement reflected genuine conviction or portfolio diversification dynamics was not disclosed in any public statement by Berkshire at the time.

What the raise does not establish: whether the capital will be deployed efficiently, whether the capacity constraints Alphabet cited are temporary supply-chain bottlenecks or persistent gaps between AI ambition and build pace, and whether the revenue growth required to justify $180 to $190 billion in annual capital expenditures will materialize on the timeline investors were pricing at the time of the raise.
