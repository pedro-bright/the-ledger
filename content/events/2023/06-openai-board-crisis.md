## Summary

OpenAI's board of directors abruptly fired CEO Sam Altman on November 17, 2023, triggering five days of corporate chaos that saw a staff revolt threatening mass resignation, Microsoft offering to hire the entire company, and ultimately Altman's reinstatement with a restructured board. The crisis exposed fundamental tensions between OpenAI's nonprofit safety mission and its commercial trajectory.

## What Happened

On the afternoon of Friday, November 17, 2023, OpenAI's board of directors announced that it had fired Sam Altman as CEO, stating he "was not consistently candid in his communications with the board." President Greg Brockman was simultaneously removed from the board and resigned in solidarity. CTO Mira Murati was named interim CEO.

What followed was one of the most dramatic corporate crises in technology history. Within 48 hours:

- Over 700 of OpenAI's approximately 770 employees signed a letter threatening to resign and follow Altman to Microsoft unless the board reversed its decision.
- Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella announced that Altman and Brockman would lead a new advanced AI research team at Microsoft, while simultaneously stating that Microsoft remained committed to its OpenAI partnership.
- Interim CEO Mira Murati was replaced by former Twitch CEO Emmett Shear, who served for roughly two days.
- Intense negotiations led to a resolution: Altman returned as CEO on November 22 with a new board consisting of Bret Taylor (chair), Larry Summers, and Adam D'Angelo (the only original board member retained).

Board members Helen Toner and Tasha McCauley, along with chief scientist Ilya Sutskever (who had initially supported the firing before signing the employee letter), departed. The nonprofit board that had governed OpenAI since its founding was fundamentally restructured.

## Why It Matters

The OpenAI board crisis was the most visible manifestation of the tension between AI safety governance and commercial AI development. The nonprofit board, designed to ensure OpenAI's mission of "safe and beneficial" AGI remained paramount, attempted to exercise its oversight authority — and was essentially overridden by economic reality within days.

The episode demonstrated that once an AI company reaches a certain scale of investment, revenue, and employee count, traditional governance mechanisms may be insufficient to check commercial momentum. Microsoft's $13 billion investment and the employees' economic interests in OpenAI's valuation created forces that overwhelmed a six-person nonprofit board.

The crisis accelerated OpenAI's evolution away from its unusual nonprofit structure. In subsequent months, the company would explore conversion to a for-profit entity, validating critics who had argued the hybrid structure was always unstable.

For the broader AI field, the episode raised uncomfortable questions: if the one company with explicit governance structures for prioritizing safety over profit couldn't make that trade-off in practice, what did that say about the industry's ability to self-regulate?
